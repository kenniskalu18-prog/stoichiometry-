The Law of Conservation of Mass

Atoms are neither created nor destroyed in chemical reactions. This fundamental principle is the basis for balancing chemical equations!

Chemical Equations: The Language of Chemistry

Chemical Equation: A shorthand way of describing a chemical reaction using symbols and formulae to represent elements and compounds involved.

Combustion of Methane

CH4 + 2O2 ‚Üí CO2 + 2H2O

Reactants: CH4 and O2 (left side of arrow)

Products: CO2 and H2O (right side of arrow)

Key Principle: In a chemical reaction, atoms are neither created nor destroyed. There must be the same number of each type of atom on both sides of the equation!

Stoichiometric Calculations: The Mathematics of Chemistry

Stoichiometry: The calculation of quantities in chemical reactions based on the balanced equation.

The Mole Ratio Bridge

Balanced equations provide mole ratios that connect reactants and products.

Example: Propane Combustion

C3H8(g) + 5O2(g) ‚Üí 3CO2(g) + 4H2O(g)

Mole Ratios:

  • 1 mole C3H8 : 5 moles O2
  • 1 mole C3H8 : 3 moles CO2
  • 1 mole C3H8 : 4 moles H2O
  • 5 moles O2 : 3 moles CO2

Sample Calculation

Problem: What mass of oxygen will react with 96.1 g of propane?

Step 1: Write the balanced equation

C3H8 + 5O2 ‚Üí 3CO2 + 4H2O

Step 2: Determine mole ratio from balanced equation

1 mole C3H8 reacts with 5 moles O2

Step 3: Convert masses to moles

Molar mass C3H8 = 44.1 g/mol

Molar mass O2 = 32.0 g/mol

5 moles O2 = 5 √ó 32.0 = 160 g

Step 4: Set up proportion and solve

44.1 g C3H8 reacts with 160 g O2

96.1 g C3H8 reacts with X g O2

X = (160 √ó 96.1) / 44.1 = 349 g O2

Limiting Reagent: The Reactant That Runs Out First

Limiting Reagent: The reactant that is completely consumed in a chemical reaction, limiting the amount of product formed.

The Toast Sandwich Analogy

If making a toast requires 2 bread slices and 1 egg, how many toasts can you make with 20 slices of bread and 12 eggs?

2Bd + 1 egg ‚Üí Bd2egg

Bread is limiting: 20 slices make only 10 toasts, despite having 12 eggs!

Example: Ammonia Synthesis

N2(g) + 3H2(g) ‚Üí 2NH3(g)

Problem: If we have 35.0 kg of N2 and 15 kg of H2, which is the limiting reactant?

Step 1: Convert masses to moles

Molar mass N2 = 28 g/mol, H2 = 2 g/mol

Moles N2 = 35,000 g / 28 g/mol = 1250 mol

Moles H2 = 15,000 g / 2 g/mol = 7500 mol

Step 2: Use mole ratio from balanced equation

Required ratio: 1 mol N2 : 3 mol H2

For 1250 mol N2, needed H2 = 1250 √ó 3 = 3750 mol

Step 3: Compare available vs. needed

Available H2: 7500 mol

Needed H2: 3750 mol

Since we have more than enough H2, N2 is the limiting reagent!

Percentage Yield: Measuring Reaction Efficiency

Theoretical Yield: The maximum amount of product that can be formed from the limiting reactant.

Actual Yield: The amount of product actually obtained in an experiment.

Percentage Yield: (Actual Yield / Theoretical Yield) √ó 100%

% Yield = (Actual Yield / Theoretical Yield) √ó 100%

Example: Lithium Nitride Formation

6Li(s) + N2(g) ‚Üí 2Li3N(s)

Problem: When 12.3 g of Li are heated with 33.6 g of N2:

a) What is the theoretical yield of Li3N?

b) If the actual yield is 5.89 g, what is the percentage yield?

Step 1: Identify limiting reagent

Molar mass Li = 6.9 g/mol, N2 = 28 g/mol, Li3N = 34.8 g/mol

Moles Li = 12.3 / 6.9 = 1.78 mol

Moles N2 = 33.6 / 28 = 1.2 mol

From equation: 6 mol Li requires 1 mol N2

For 1.78 mol Li, needed N2 = 1.78/6 = 0.297 mol

We have 1.2 mol N2, so Li is limiting

Step 2: Calculate theoretical yield

6 mol Li produces 2 mol Li3N

1.78 mol Li produces (2/6) √ó 1.78 = 0.593 mol Li3N

Theoretical yield = 0.593 √ó 34.8 = 20.6 g Li3N

Step 3: Calculate percentage yield

% Yield = (5.89 / 20.6) √ó 100% = 28.6%

Why yields are less than 100%: Side reactions, incomplete reactions, product loss during purification, and measurement errors all contribute to actual yields being lower than theoretical yields.

Problem 1: Sodium bicarbonate reacts with citric acid: NaHCO3(aq) + C6H8O7(aq) ‚Üí CO2(g) + H2O(l) + Na3C6H5O7(aq)

a) Balance the equation.

b) What mass of C6H8O7 should be used for every 0.05 g of NaHCO3?

Solution:

a) 3NaHCO3 + C6H8O7 ‚Üí 3CO2 + 3H2O + Na3C6H5O7

b) Molar mass NaHCO3 = 84 g/mol, C6H8O7 = 192 g/mol

From equation: 3 mol NaHCO3 reacts with 1 mol C6H8O7

0.05 g NaHCO3 = 0.05/84 = 0.000595 mol

Moles C6H8O7 needed = 0.000595/3 = 0.000198 mol

Mass = 0.000198 √ó 192 = 0.038 g

Problem 2: Octane combustion: 2C8H18(l) + 25O2(g) ‚Üí 16CO2(g) + 18H2O(l)

a) How many moles of CO2 are produced when 2.0 √ó 1010 mol of C8H18 is burnt?

b) How many moles of oxygen are required to burn 4.4 √ó 1010 mol of C8H18?

Solution:

a) From equation: 2 mol C8H18 produces 16 mol CO2

Mole ratio: 1:8

CO2 produced = 2.0 √ó 1010 √ó 8 = 1.6 √ó 1011 mol

b) From equation: 2 mol C8H18 requires 25 mol O2

Mole ratio: 1:12.5

O2 required = 4.4 √ó 1010 √ó 12.5 = 5.5 √ó 1011 mol

Problem 3: Nitric oxide reacts with oxygen: 2NO(g) + O2(g) ‚Üí 2NO2(g)

If 0.886 mole of NO is mixed with 0.503 mole of O2:

a) Which is the limiting reagent?

b) How many moles of NO2 are produced?

Solution:

a) From equation: 2 mol NO requires 1 mol O2

For 0.886 mol NO, needed O2 = 0.886/2 = 0.443 mol

Available O2 = 0.503 mol (more than needed)

Therefore, NO is the limiting reagent

b) From equation: 2 mol NO produces 2 mol NO2 (1:1 ratio)

NO2 produced = 0.886 mol

Common Pitfalls and Expert Tips ‚ö†Ô∏è

Pitfall 1: Changing Subscripts Instead of Coefficients

When balancing equations, never change the subscripts in chemical formulas (like H2O to H2O2). Only change coefficients in front of formulas.

Pitfall 2: Forgetting to Balance the Equation First

Always balance the chemical equation before attempting any stoichiometric calculations. Unbalanced equations give incorrect mole ratios!

Pitfall 3: Confusing Limiting Reagent with Excess Reagent

The limiting reagent is completely consumed, while the excess reagent is leftover after the reaction. Always calculate which one runs out first.

Tip 1: Use the "Atom Inventory" Method for Balancing

Create a table counting atoms on each side. Start balancing with the most complicated molecule (usually the one with the most atoms).

Tip 2: Memorize Key Molar Masses

Know common molar masses: H2O = 18 g/mol, CO2 = 44 g/mol, O2 = 32 g/mol, N2 = 28 g/mol. This speeds up calculations.

Tip 3: Always Include Units in Calculations

Writing units (g, mol, g/mol) helps track what you're calculating and catches errors when units don't cancel properly.
